Biomes and Food Security: The Murray-Darling Basin Under Pressure

Geography · Year 9 · Biomes and food security · ~70 min
AC9HG9K02AC9HG9K04

Learning intentions

  • Explain why the Murray-Darling Basin is significant to Australia's food security
  • Identify the environmental and human pressures reducing water security in the Basin
  • Evaluate a proposed response to a food/water security challenge

Success criteria

  • I can state, with figures, how much of Australia's agricultural production depends on the Murray-Darling Basin
  • I can explain at least two causes of water security loss in the Basin using a named source
  • I can assess one management response and identify a trade-off it involves

Content descriptors covered

  • AC9HG9K02 The human alteration of biomes to produce food, industrial materials and fibres, and the use of continents, biotechnology and fishing grounds
  • AC9HG9K04 The challenges to food production, including land and water degradation, competing land uses, and climate change, and the food security implications for Australia and the world

Key vocabulary

Food security
Reliable access to enough safe, affordable and nutritious food.
Water security
Reliable access to an acceptable quantity and quality of water for health, livelihoods and production.
Biome alteration
Human modification of a natural biome (e.g. clearing, irrigating, damming) to increase its use for food or fibre production.
Over-allocation
When more water is licensed for extraction from a river system than the system can sustainably provide.

Starter activity

Look at a food label from something in your kitchen (or picture one: pasta, bread, rice, wine, cotton clothing). Guess: could any of the ingredients or materials have come from the Murray-Darling Basin? Jot down your guess and one reason for it, then compare with a partner.

Lesson sequence

1. Starter: where does it come from? 8 min

Run the starter activity above as a think-pair-share. Cold-call two or three pairs to share their guesses before revealing that the Murray-Darling Basin produces about one third of Australia's food supply and around 40% of the gross value of national agricultural production (CSIRO).

Your task: Guess and justify which everyday food or fibre products might come from the Murray-Darling Basin.

2. Locating and sizing the Basin 10 min

Show a map of the Murray-Darling Basin (it covers parts of Queensland, New South Wales, Victoria, South Australia and the ACT). Students annotate a blank outline map with: the Basin's approximate size, the states it crosses, and 2-3 major towns/cities that rely on it.

Your task: Annotate your map with the Basin's extent and at least three settlements inside it.

3. Reading: how the Basin was altered for food production 15 min

In pairs, students read the CSIRO Basin overview and the water-security article (linked in Sources below). While reading, they complete a two-column note: 'How the Basin has been altered for agriculture' and 'Pressures reducing its water security'.

Your task: Complete the two-column reading notes using evidence and figures from both CSIRO sources.

4. Case discussion: drought and over-allocation 15 min

Introduce the Millennium Drought (1997-2009) and the 2017-2019 drought as case evidence of reduced water security. Facilitate a class discussion: Why might upstream irrigators, downstream towns, and river ecosystems want different things from the same water? Introduce the term 'over-allocation'.

Your task: In your notes, list at least three different groups who rely on Murray-Darling Basin water and what each group needs from it.

5. Evaluate a response 15 min

Present the Murray-Darling Basin Plan (a real government response that sets legal limits on how much water can be taken from the system) as one management response. In small groups, students evaluate it using a simple trade-off frame: Who benefits? Who might lose out? What questions would you still want answered?

Your task: Complete a trade-off table for the Murray-Darling Basin Plan: benefits, costs/trade-offs, and one unanswered question.

6. Share and consolidate 7 min

Groups share one row from their trade-off table. Draw out the general geographic idea: managing a shared resource for food security often means balancing competing human and environmental needs.

Your task: Contribute one insight from your group's trade-off table to the class discussion.

Sources used in this lesson

  • Murray–Darling Basin CSIRO

    CSIRO's overview of the Basin explains that it generates about 40% (roughly $15 billion) of the gross value of Australia's agricultural production and supplies water to over 2.4 million people, including more than 40 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander nations.

  • Enhancing water security in the Murray-Darling Basin CSIRO

    Describes how increasing demand on the Basin's naturally variable flows, combined with climate change, has reduced water security for communities, industries and the environment, and outlines the science informing the Murray-Darling Basin Plan review.

  • Murray–Darling basin Wikipedia

    A general reference on the Basin's geography, climate and history, useful for locating the Basin on a map and for background on the Millennium Drought (1997–2009) and the 2017–2019 drought.

Differentiation

Support: Provide a partially completed two-column note template with sentence starters ('The Basin has been altered by...', 'One pressure on water security is...'). Pair students with a stronger reader for the source-reading step.

Extension: Ask students to research one other food-producing region in the world facing similar water security pressures (e.g. California's Central Valley, the Aral Sea basin) and write a short comparison paragraph.

Check your understanding

Q1. Approximately what share of Australia's gross agricultural production value comes from the Murray-Darling Basin?

  • About 10%
  • About 25%
  • About 40%
  • About 75%
Show answer

About 40% — CSIRO states the Basin generates roughly 40% (about $15 billion) of the gross value of Australia's agricultural production.

Q2. What does 'water over-allocation' mean, and why is it a problem for food security?

Show answer

Over-allocation is when more water is licensed for use than the river system can sustainably supply. It threatens food security because it can leave too little water for reliable irrigation in dry years, as well as damaging the river ecosystems that agriculture and communities depend on long-term.

Q3. Name one group of water users in the Murray-Darling Basin and one thing they need from the river system.

Show answer

Accept any reasonable answer, e.g. irrigators need reliable water for crops; river communities need drinking water; Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander nations need water for cultural and environmental values; downstream ecosystems need environmental flows to stay healthy.

Exit ticket

In one or two sentences: why can't every group that wants water from the Murray-Darling Basin simply get as much as they ask for?
